# FINS3616 Midterm Study Repository

## Purpose

This repository organises all course materials for **FINS3616** in preparation for the midterm examination.

The repository is designed to support two main uses:

1. **Generate complete weekly study notes** by synthesising lecture slides, lecture transcripts, textbook material, tutorial questions, tutorial solutions, and additional practice resources.
2. **Answer course questions accurately** using the uploaded course materials as the primary source of truth.

The aim is not to summarise each file separately. The aim is to combine all materials for each week into one clear and complete understanding of the examinable content.

## Types of Files Included

### 1. Lecture Slides

Lecture slides provide the main structure of the course and identify the key examinable concepts, formulas, definitions, models, diagrams, and examples.

Use the lecture slides to determine:

- the main topics for the week;
- the order in which concepts should be explained;
- key formulas and notation;
- important diagrams and relationships;
- lecturer-selected examples;
- concepts likely to be assessed.

Lecture slides should form the **main framework** for all weekly notes.

---

### 2. Lecture Transcripts

Lecture transcripts provide the detailed explanation behind the slides.

Use the transcripts to identify:

- the lecturer’s explanation of difficult concepts;
- reasoning that may not appear on the slides;
- verbal examples;
- warnings about common mistakes;
- comments about what is important for tutorials or exams;
- explanations of formulas and derivations;
- links between different concepts;
- interpretation of graphs, models, and results.

Where the transcript and slides cover the same topic, combine them into one clear explanation rather than treating them separately.

If Lecture A and Lecture B transcripts are both available, use both. They may explain the same topic differently or include different examples.

---

### 3. Textbook Chapters and Testbank Material

Textbook chapters provide formal theory, definitions, derivations, and additional examples.

Use the textbook to:

- support and expand the lecture content;
- provide formal definitions;
- explain assumptions;
- clarify mathematical steps;
- supply additional examples;
- confirm formulas and terminology;
- provide deeper background where the lecture material is brief.

Testbank questions should be used to identify recurring question styles and to create additional exam practice.

The textbook should support the lecture material, but it should not replace the lecture emphasis.

---

### 4. Tutorial Questions

Tutorial questions show how the weekly theory is applied.

Use tutorial questions to identify:

- the main calculation types;
- common proof or derivation questions;
- interpretation questions;
- likely exam-style applications;
- recurring formulas;
- common assumptions;
- the steps required to solve problems.

Tutorial questions should be treated as one of the strongest indicators of what students are expected to understand and apply.

---

### 5. Tutorial Solutions

Tutorial solutions provide the expected method, working, notation, and final answer format.

Use tutorial solutions to:

- verify answers;
- identify the preferred solving method;
- understand the required level of working;
- identify shortcuts or standard approaches;
- compare alternative methods;
- learn how marks may be awarded;
- identify common traps and assumptions.

Do not copy solutions without explanation. Every solution should be converted into a repeatable method that can be applied to new questions.

---

### 6. Additional Questions, Proofs, Comments, and Worked Solutions

Additional materials provide deeper clarification and extra practice.

Use these documents to:

- reinforce difficult topics;
- identify alternative question formats;
- study proofs and derivations;
- understand edge cases;
- practise concepts beyond the standard tutorial;
- resolve gaps between the lecture and tutorial material.

These materials should support the main weekly synthesis rather than be summarised independently.

## Core Synthesis Rule

Do not create separate summaries of every document.

Instead, for each week:

1. Identify the main topics from the lecture slides.
2. Expand each topic using the lecture transcripts.
3. Support the explanation using the textbook.
4. connect the theory to the tutorial questions;
5. use the tutorial solutions to explain the correct method;
6. incorporate relevant additional questions, proofs, and comments;
7. combine all sources into one integrated set of notes.

The final notes should read as though they were written from a complete understanding of the entire week, not as a collection of file summaries.

## Required Weekly Note Structure

Each weekly set of notes should follow the structure below.

# Week X — Topic Name

## 1. Weekly Overview

Provide a short overview covering:

- the main topics;
- how the topics connect;
- the key skills students are expected to develop;
- the main tutorial applications;
- the likely examinable areas.

## 2. Learning Objectives

List what a student should be able to:

- define;
- explain;
- calculate;
- derive;
- interpret;
- compare;
- apply.

## 3. Key Concepts and Theory

For each concept, include:

### Concept Name

**Definition**

Provide a precise definition using the lecture and textbook material.

**Intuition**

Explain the concept in plain language.

**Why It Matters**

Explain its role in the course and why it is used.

**Assumptions**

List all assumptions required for the concept, model, formula, or result.

**Key Relationships**

Explain how the concept connects to other topics.

**Lecture Emphasis**

Include any lecturer comments, warnings, shortcuts, interpretations, or exam guidance.

---

## 4. Formulas and Notation

For every formula, include:

- the complete formula;
- the meaning of each variable;
- units where relevant;
- assumptions;
- when the formula applies;
- when the formula should not be used;
- how to rearrange it;
- how to interpret the result.

Example:

```text
Formula:
[Insert formula]

Where:
- X = ...
- Y = ...
- Z = ...

Use when:
...

Do not use when:
...

Interpretation:
...
```

---

## 5. Derivations and Proofs

Where the course includes derivations or proofs:

1. state the result;
2. identify the starting assumptions;
3. show each algebraic or logical step;
4. explain why each step is valid;
5. state the final conclusion;
6. explain the intuition behind the result;
7. identify whether the full derivation is likely examinable.

Do not skip mathematical steps unless they are genuinely trivial.

---

## 6. Diagrams, Graphs, and Models

For every important diagram or graph, explain:

- the axes;
- variables;
- curves or lines;
- direction of movement;
- equilibrium or key points;
- comparative statics;
- interpretation;
- common mistakes.

Where a diagram cannot be reproduced, provide a clear written description of how it should be drawn.

---

## 7. Tutorial Applications

Organise tutorial questions by concept or question type.

For each question type, include:

### Question Type

**What the Question Is Testing**

Explain the underlying concept.

**General Method**

Provide a reusable step-by-step process.

**Worked Application**

Use a tutorial or additional question as an example.

**Interpretation**

Explain the meaning of the final answer.

**Common Mistakes**

Identify likely errors.

**Exam Variation**

Explain how the same concept could be asked differently.

---

## 8. Standard Problem-Solving Methods

Convert tutorial solutions into reusable procedures.

Example format:

```text
Method: Solving [Question Type]

Step 1: Identify ...
Step 2: Write ...
Step 3: Substitute ...
Step 4: Rearrange ...
Step 5: Calculate ...
Step 6: Interpret ...
Step 7: Check ...
```

These methods should be general enough to apply to unseen exam questions.

---

## 9. Common Mistakes and Traps

Include mistakes identified through:

- lecturer warnings;
- tutorial solutions;
- incorrect assumptions;
- sign errors;
- notation errors;
- confusing similar formulas;
- using the wrong model;
- failing to interpret a result;
- skipping required working;
- rounding too early.

For each mistake, explain how to avoid it.

---

## 10. Exam-Focused Summary

Provide a concise summary of:

- concepts that must be memorised;
- formulas that must be known;
- derivations that must be understood;
- question types that must be practised;
- interpretations that must be explained;
- common traps;
- high-priority revision areas.

Create a set of practice questions based on the weekly material.

Include a mixture of:

- definitions;

- derivations;

## Question-Answering Instructions

When answering a question using this repository:

1. Identify the relevant week and topic.
2. Search the lecture slides and transcripts first.
3. Check whether the topic appears in tutorial questions.
4. Use tutorial solutions to verify the required method.
5. Use the textbook and additional documents for support.
6. Clearly separate course-based information from any outside explanation.
7. Do not invent missing information.
8. Show all relevant working.
9. Explain both the method and the intuition.
10. State assumptions.
11. Interpret the final answer.
12. Cite the source file or files used.

## Citation and Traceability Rules

Every generated note or answer should identify where the information came from.

Use citations such as:

```text
Source: Week 3 Lecture A transcript
Source: Week 3 Chapter 4 lecture slides, slide 18
Source: Week 3 Tutorial Question 4
Source: Week 3 Tutorial Solutions, Question 4
Source: Testbank Chapter 4, Question 12
```

Where multiple files support the same explanation, cite all relevant files.

Do not claim that a point came from the lecturer unless it appears in a lecture slide or transcript.

---

## Handling Conflicts Between Documents

If two files appear inconsistent:

1. compare the date and version of each file;
2. prioritise updated or final documents;
3. prioritise lecture and official tutorial material;
4. determine whether the difference is due to notation, rounding, assumptions, or method;
5. explain the inconsistency clearly;
6. do not silently choose one answer;
7. state which version is being followed and why.

Files labelled **Final**, **Updated**, or **Revised** should generally be prioritised over older versions.

---

## Handling Missing Information

If the answer cannot be supported by the repository:

- state that the information was not found;
- identify which files were checked;
- explain what additional material would be required;
- do not fabricate an answer;
- do not present outside knowledge as course content.

Outside knowledge may only be added when clearly labelled:

```text
Additional explanation not directly stated in the uploaded course materials:
...
```
